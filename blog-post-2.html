Treadmill vs Rowing Machine: Which Is Better for Fat Loss in 2025?

A head-to-head comparison of calories burned, muscle activation, joint impact, and value for money — so you can pick the right machine for your goals.

Both treadmills and rowing machines are outstanding tools for fat loss — but they work very differently. The right choice depends on your fitness level, injury history, goals, and budget. In this guide we pit them head-to-head across every key factor so you can make a confident decision.

Calories Burned: Who Wins?

Calorie burn depends heavily on intensity and body weight, but here's a useful comparison for a 155 lb (70 kg) person at moderate intensity over 30 minutes:

  • Treadmill (running at 6 mph): ~300–370 calories
  • Rowing machine (moderate effort): ~260–316 calories
  • Rowing machine (vigorous effort): ~370–440 calories

At moderate intensity, treadmill running edges ahead. But at vigorous rowing intensity — which engages far more muscle groups — rowing can actually burn more calories per session. The key difference is that rowing works approximately 86% of muscles in the body vs. around 50–60% for running, which means more total metabolic demand when effort is matched.

Muscle Activation

This is where rowing wins clearly. A proper rowing stroke activates legs (60%), core (20%), and upper body (20%) in a coordinated full-body movement. Treadmill running is primarily lower body with some core stabilisation. If you're after fat loss combined with muscle preservation — especially in the upper body — rowing has a significant edge.

Joint Impact

Treadmills involve repetitive impact loading through the knees, hips, and ankles — particularly at higher speeds. Modern treadmills have cushioning systems that reduce impact by 15–40% vs road running, but for anyone with joint sensitivities, it's still worth considering. Rowing is completely low-impact, making it suitable for those recovering from lower body injuries or who need to protect their joints long-term.

"If joint health is a concern, rowing's zero-impact nature gives it a significant long-term advantage over treadmill running."

Which Is Easier to Learn?

Treadmills win on simplicity — get on, set a speed, walk or run. Almost anyone can use a treadmill on day one. Rowing has a learning curve. The catch, drive, finish, and recovery phases of the stroke take several sessions to develop proper form. Poor rowing technique reduces efficiency and can strain the lower back. Most gyms and rowing brands provide technique videos — the Concept2 YouTube channel is excellent for this.

Price Comparison

Entry-level treadmills start around $300–$500, but quality home treadmills (suitable for regular running) tend to start around $800–$1,200. The NordicTrack Commercial 1750 at around $1,499 is one of the best value premium options. Rowing machines are generally better value — the Concept2 Model D at around $900 is considered the industry gold standard and will last decades. Budget rowers start from $300.

Space Requirements

Both machines require meaningful floor space. A typical treadmill needs roughly 6.5 ft x 3 ft of floor space, with additional clearance behind for safety. A rowing machine needs approximately 8–9 ft x 2 ft when in use, but can often be stored vertically (the Concept2 Model D folds and stands upright), making it more garage/apartment friendly.

Which Should You Buy?

Choose a treadmill if you: enjoy running or walking, need something immediately intuitive, want to train for road races, or primarily want lower body cardiovascular conditioning.

Choose a rowing machine if you: want a full-body workout, have joint concerns, are on a tighter budget (quality for money is better), or want something that doubles as a strength endurance tool.

If budget and space allow — get both. Used together, they provide the most complete cardiovascular training programme possible from home.
